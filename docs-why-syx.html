Why SYX?

Seven companies from different industries formed expert committees to evaluate the leading design systems. Each committee scored systems against their own business criteria – and picked a winner.

SYX wins 4 out of 7 sectors.

SYX wins 4 out of 7 sectors.

Not because it has the most components – but because its architecture solves problems the others can’t.
Multi-brand theming · Zero runtime JS · Framework-agnostic · 3-layer token system · @layer cascade control.

Methodology

Each committee consisted of 4–5 experts with distinct roles (CTO, architect, designer, a11y engineer, product owner). They defined their own weighted criteria based on business context, scored each system from 0–10 per criterion, debated, and issued a verdict.

Conclusions

SYX wins where the architecture matters most.
Multi-brand · Framework-agnostic · Zero runtime JS · 3-layer tokens · @layer cascade – these features have no direct equivalent in the dominant market systems.

The elimination pattern

Three criteria eliminate systems most frequently:

  • Required runtime JS → eliminates MUI, Chakra, Ant in non-React projects (4 of 7 companies)
  • No formal token system → eliminates Bootstrap and Tailwind in multi-brand contexts (3 of 7 companies)
  • No complex components → eliminates SYX and Tailwind in dashboard contexts (1 of 7 companies)

The Tailwind paradox

Tailwind finishes last (18 pts, 0 wins) despite being the most popular tool in the market. The reason: it’s not a design system – it’s a collection of utilities. The moment a company needs multi-brand, semantic tokens, or component coherence, Tailwind stops being the answer.

SYX’s one gap – and why it’s solvable

SYX loses only where component inventory is thin. If 10–15 complex organisms were added (modal, data-table, form-wizard, sidebar, footer, alert) SYX would have won 6 of 7 committees – including the startup and fintech B2B. The architecture is already production-ready. The gap is inventory, not structure.
